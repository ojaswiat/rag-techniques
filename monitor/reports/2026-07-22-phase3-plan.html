Risks

  • Real, tracked deviation: Phase 3's Groq calls bypass call_groq()'s backoff/semaphore. Burst rate within one filing's build (dozens of summarization calls in quick succession) carries genuine 429 exposure even though total volume across 9 filings is small — TPM/RPM risk, not daily-volume risk.
  • Can cost real time or money if unmitigated: an unhandled 429 mid-build could corrupt a filing's cache and force a manual re-run; repeated failures eating into the free-tier daily cap could force an earlier-than-planned move to Groq's paid Developer tier.
  • Mitigations are written into the plan, not yet proven live: sequential builds, atomic temp-dir-then-rename, TokenCountingHandler cost logging — all covered by mocked unit tests in the plan, but the real risk is only retired once Task 6's live throttled run (Step 5) actually succeeds against Groq.
  • Phase 3's full 9-filing release additionally depends on Phase 2's still-outstanding 6-filing ingestion (MSFT ×3, TSLA ×3) — tracked in temp/deferred.md, not this plan's problem to solve but a real blocker before the full (non-throttled) release.
